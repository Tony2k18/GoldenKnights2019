WHY MESS WITH MAGIC?

Following the Golden Knights into 2020

T he Golden Knights made some noise during the off season as they retooled a roster in need of subtle upgrades, but they didn’t touch the trio most responsible for the championship dust sprinkled across their first year of existence. William Karlsson. Jonathan Marchessault. Reilly Smith. Together, they form the best forward line in the NHL (OK, arguably).

Profiles of Karlsson, Marchessault and Smith appear inside, along with a story that delves into what makes them click as a unit. Last year’s preseason editions proved popular with fans and collectors, and this year’s four sections again deliver features, analyses and insights as the Knights enter Year 2 and look to defend their Western Conference championship and make another run at theStanley Cup.

Jonathan Marchessault CAREER STATS

Marchessault is a big reason the Vegas Golden Knights became the best first-year franchise in NHL history. The Golden Knights selected the native of Cap-Rouge, Quebec, from the Florida Panthers in the 2017 NHL Expansion Draft after he scored 30 goals in 2016-17. He quickly showed that was no fluke, finding a home on the first line with William Karlsson and Reilly Smith. He finished with an NHL career-best 75 points, second to Karlsson, and was rewarded with a five-year, $30 million contract on Jan. 3, 2018.

Reeves made sure the first goal he scored for the Vegas Golden Knights was one for the record books. Reilly Smith CAREER STATS

Smith was a big reason the Florida Panthers, coached by Gerard Gallant, won the Atlantic Division title in 2015-16. Two seasons later, he played a key role in helping the Vegas Golden Knights to the best finish by a first-year team in NHL history - again on a team coached by Gallant. Smith, a right wing and the brother of New York Rangers defenseman Brendan Smith, teamed with center William Karlsson and left wing Jonathan Marchessault to form one of the NHL's most effective lines. He finished with NHL career highs in assists (38) and points (60), helping Vegas win the Pacific Division.

When Stone arrived at Ottawa Senators training camp in September 2014, nothing special was expected of the 22-year-old forward. Four goals and eight points certainly weren't much of an indication that Stone was a budding NHL star. But during the next eight months, Stone not only developed into a star, he became something of a phenomenon.
